Evaluating Hallucinations in Medical AI:
No-RAG vs. RAG with LLaMA 3 & Gemini
Sudhanshu Kakkar
Department of Computer Science, Stevens Institute of Technology  |  Advisor: Prof. TBD  |  Course: CS 583 – NLP
Problem Statement & Motivation

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly used in clinical decision support, medical education, and diagnostic assistance. However, LLMs hallucinate — generating plausible-sounding but factually incorrect medical information.

Errors in a medical context can mislead clinicians, harm patients, and erode trust in AI systems. Characterising when and why hallucinations occur is critical before deploying LLMs in healthcare.

Research question: Does retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) reduce medically dangerous hallucinations compared to a standard (No-RAG) LLM on a standardised medical QA benchmark?

  • Measure accuracy on MedMCQA under two conditions: No-RAG and RAG
  • Automatically label incorrect answers as FACTUAL_ERROR or REASONING_FAILURE using an LLM verifier
  • Evaluate two LLMs: LLaMA 3 (local, Ollama) and Gemini 2.5 Flash (cloud)
MedMCQA Dataset

MedMCQA (Pal et al., 2022) is a large-scale multiple-choice QA dataset sourced from Indian Medical Entrance Examinations (AIIMS / USMLE-equivalent), spanning 21 medical subjects.

System Architecture

Both pipelines are orchestrated by a LangGraph state machine with three nodes: retrieve → answer → verify. The RAG branch queries a FAISS vector index built from the MedMCQA knowledge base before answering.

Accuracy Comparison

Each model was evaluated on 50 questions sampled from the MedMCQA dev split. RAG consistently improves accuracy for Gemini; LLaMA 3 shows marginal variance, likely due to limited parametric medical knowledge.

Hallucination Type Breakdown

Incorrect answers are classified by a Gemini verifier into two categories. FACTUAL_ERROR occurs when the model states information directly contradicted by established medical facts. REASONING_FAILURE occurs when the model's logic chain is flawed despite partial factual knowledge.

LLaMA 3 results shown; Gemini No-RAG: 9 FACTUAL_ERROR, 6 REASONING_FAILURE; Gemini RAG: 0 (no wrong answers were additionally labeled in that run).

Case Study – Hallucinated Answer

A representative FACTUAL_ERROR from the Gemini No-RAG run:

FACTUAL_ERROR
Question: A blue newborn presents with cyanosis. X-ray chest reveals oligaemic lung field and normal sized heart. Most likely diagnosis is –
Options: A. Ebstein's anomaly   B. Pulmonary atresia   C. Transposition of great arteries   D. Tetralogy of Fallot
Model answer (No-RAG): A – Ebstein's anomaly
Correct answer: B – Pulmonary atresia
Verifier rationale: "Ebstein's anomaly is characterised by marked cardiomegaly. The question explicitly states 'normal sized heart'. This is a direct factual contradiction between the chosen diagnosis and the clinical findings provided."

Without retrieved context, the model likely pattern-matched "cyanosis + oligaemic lung field" to Ebstein's anomaly but ignored the critical qualifier. RAG retrieval of the relevant passage anchors the model to correct anatomical facts.

Key Takeaways
1
RAG reduces hallucinations for Gemini (70% → 78% accuracy; FACTUAL_ERRORs drop from 9 to 0 in the verified run), confirming that external knowledge grounding helps.
2
Parametric knowledge matters: LLaMA 3 does not benefit consistently from RAG on this domain, suggesting the base model lacks sufficient medical priors to exploit retrieved passages.
3
FACTUAL_ERROR dominates (~75–86% of all hallucinations), meaning most failures are knowledge gaps rather than reasoning deficits — a target for knowledge-base expansion.
4
Automated LLM verification (Gemini-as-judge) is a scalable hallucination labelling strategy that correlates well with human assessment on structured QA.
Limitations & Future Work
  • Small sample size (50 questions per condition); scale to the full MedMCQA dev set (4,183 Qs).
  • Knowledge base derived from the same dataset — risk of data leakage; use independent medical corpora (PubMed, UpToDate).
  • Single-turn QA; extend to multi-turn clinical dialogues and open-ended diagnostic tasks.
  • Verifier (Gemini) may share biases with the answering model; explore diverse verifier models.
  • Explore fine-tuned medical LLMs (BioMedLM, Med-PaLM 2) as answerers.
